Assata Shakur

Influence on Art

Assata Shakur, born JoAnne Deborah Byron on July 16, 1947, in New York City, is an iconic figure in Black liberation history. Raised between New York and North Carolina, she was profoundly shaped by the harsh realities of racism and segregation in America. Her early experiences with discrimination instilled in her a strong sense of justice and resistance.

Shakur’s journey into activism began during the 1960s, a period marked by the Civil Rights Movement and widespread social upheaval. She joined the Black Panther Party (BPP), an organization committed to the protection and empowerment of Black communities. Later, she became a member of the Black Liberation Army (BLA), which engaged in armed struggle against systemic racism and police brutality.

In 1973, Shakur was involved in a highly publicized incident on the New Jersey Turnpike, where a shootout occurred between BLA members and state troopers. She was accused of killing a state trooper, convicted in a controversial trial that critics argue was marred by racial bias, and sentenced to life in prison. In 1979, with the help of her supporters, she escaped from prison and fled to Cuba, where she was granted political asylum and continues to live in exile.

Shakur is also a powerful writer, with her autobiography, Assata: An Autobiography, providing a profound account of her life, struggles, and insights. Her reflections reveal her experiences with racism and the importance of cultural identity. One of her earliest confrontations with cultural prejudice occurred in childhood when a teacher dismissed Black music:

“She didn’t recognize that we had our own music and that we loved music. For her, there was no other music except Bach and Beethoven and Mozart. To her, we were uncultured and uncouth. For her, Latin music, jazz, rhythm and blues were trashy and we were trash. She was a racist who would have denied it to the bitter end.” (Shakur 186)

Shakur’s later experiences as a teacher demonstrated her dedication to cultural education. She was horrified to see Black children drawing white, blonde figures when asked to depict themselves. Determined to counter this, she transformed her classroom:

“On the first day of the arts and crafts class i had nothing really prepared, so i asked everyone to draw themselves... I went home and ransacked every magazine i could find with pictures of Black people. I came in early the next day and plastered the walls with pictures of Black people.” (Shakur 247)

For Shakur, art was a tool of empowerment. She understood that representation mattered—that for Black children to see themselves as beautiful and valuable, they needed to see their own images celebrated. Her life’s work has influenced countless artists, poets, and activists who continue to draw inspiration from her words and legacy.
