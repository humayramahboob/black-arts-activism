Augusta Savage

Augusta Savage (1892–1962) was a pioneering African American sculptor, educator, and activist whose work and advocacy were instrumental in the Harlem Renaissance. Born in Green Cove Springs, Florida, Savage showed an early talent for sculpting, using natural clay to create her first figures. Despite facing racial and financial obstacles, she pursued her passion, eventually studying at Cooper Union in New York City.

Savage is best known for her powerful sculptures that celebrated Black culture and human dignity. Her most famous work, Lift Every Voice and Sing (The Harp) (1939), was a monumental sculpture created for the 1939 New York World’s Fair, symbolizing the musical heritage of African Americans. Although the sculpture was destroyed after the fair, it remains an iconic representation of her artistic vision.

In addition to her own work, Savage was a dedicated educator and mentor to numerous young artists, founding the Savage Studio of Arts and Crafts in Harlem. She taught and inspired a generation of African American artists, including Jacob Lawrence, Norman Lewis, and Gwendolyn Knight, helping to cultivate a vibrant Black artistic community.
